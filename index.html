1.1 What is Artivive and how does it work?

Artivive is a platform that lets artists, designers, educators, and businesses add a digital layer to physical images. When someone points their phone camera at a physical artwork, poster, mural, or printed piece, the Artivive app or its browser-based WebAR technology recognises the image and displays a connected digital experience on top of it in real time.

The underlying technology relies on image recognition. When you upload an image to Artivive's creator tool, the system analyses it and maps its unique visual pattern — like a fingerprint. From that point on, any time a viewer scans that same image, the system matches it to your account and activates the digital content you have attached to it.

That digital content can be almost anything: a looping video animation, a 3D object rotating in space, layered audio, particle effects, interactive buttons, or complex multi-state experiences that respond to taps. The content appears overlaid on the physical artwork through the camera view, blending the physical and digital in real time.

Why this matters for creators: Artivive removes the technical barrier between a static physical work and a rich digital experience. You do not need to write code, work with a developer, or export to a game engine. Everything is handled inside one web-based editor.

Key vocabulary:

  • Trigger image — the physical image that activates the AR experience
  • Digital layer — any file (video, image, audio, 3D object) attached to the trigger
  • AR experience/artwork — the full interactive overlay seen through the camera
  • Artivive Editor — Artivive's creator tool, accessed via web browser or app

1.2 Creating your account

Go to bridge.artivive.com and sign up for a free Creator Account. You can register using your email address or via a social login with Google, Apple, Facebook, or Clever — whichever you prefer.

If you register by email, check your inbox immediately for a confirmation message and click the activation link. Your account will not work until it is activated. If the email doesn't appear within a few minutes, check your spam folder before contacting support.

Once activated, log in at bridge.artivive.com to access the Artivive Editor. This is where all creation happens.

What the free account includes:

  • Full access to the Artivive editor
  • The ability to create and publish unlimited AR artworks
  • 50 lifetime views — meaning your artworks can be scanned a combined total of 50 times before you need to upgrade

The free account is designed for testing and learning. Experiment with the editor, and understand the platform before committing to a paid plan.

Practical note: The 50 views are shared across all artworks you create, not 50 per artwork. If you publish three artworks and each gets scanned 20 times, you will have used all 60 views — but since the limit is 50, the 10 scans beyond that won't register. Plan your testing accordingly.

1.3 Choosing the right plan

Artivive uses a freemium model. The free tier is real and functional — not a crippled trial — but it does have limits in both view count and available features.

The plans at a glance:

Free gives you 50 lifetime views and access to the core editor. You can upload images, add video and image layers, publish, and test your work. This is where everyone starts.

Pro gives you 1,000 views per month, unlocking the ability to share your work publicly in a meaningful way. All core editing features remain available.

Pro Plus gives you 5,000 views per month and also unlocks two major advanced features: Timeline (animation) and Sequences (interactive states). These are the most powerful creative tools in the platform, and they require Pro Plus to access. WebAR — the ability to share AR experiences via QR code without the app — is also a Pro Plus feature.

Business and Custom plans are available for high-volume needs, museums, galleries, agencies, and institutions. These are negotiated directly with Artivive.

Choosing annual billing saves more than 35% compared to monthly pricing on any paid plan, and subscriptions can be changed or cancelled at any time, so you are not locked in.

1.4 Understanding the Artivive Editor

Artivive Editor is your creative workspace. Everything you build in Artivive starts and ends here. When you log in, you will see your artwork collection — a dashboard showing all the AR projects in your account.

To create a new artwork, click the "Create Artwork" button in the top right corner. This opens the editor, where you will do four main things:

Upload a trigger image. This is the image Artivive will learn to recognize. It becomes the anchor for your entire experience.

Add and arrange digital layers. You can upload your own files — video, images, audio, 3D objects — or browse the built-in elements library and pre-made templates. Layers stack on top of each other and can be moved, rotated, and scaled in both 2D and 3D view.

Animate and configure. With a Pro Plus account, you can open the Timeline to animate layers. You can also add interactions, sound settings, particle effects, and more.

Publish and preview. Before going live, Preview Mode lets you test your experience in the editor. Once you're happy, hit Publish. After processing, your artwork is live and scannable.

The editor interface is browser-based, so there is nothing to install. It works on any modern desktop browser. For editing on mobile, Artivive also offers a dedicated app with its own editing capabilities, covered in Module 2.

2.1 Understanding the trigger image

The trigger image is the most important element of any Artivive project. It is what the app or WebAR recognizes when a viewer scans it, and it determines how reliably the AR experience will activate.

Think of the trigger image as an anchor. The app does not search for objects or faces — it looks for a very specific visual pattern. When it finds a match, it overlays the connected digital content precisely aligned to that image.

Your trigger image can be:

  • A digital artwork you plan to print
  • A photograph of a physical painting, mural, or sculpture
  • A product label, book cover, poster, or magazine spread
  • Any image with enough visual complexity to be uniquely identified

When you upload a trigger image to the editor, the system analyses it and gives it a trackability rating between 0 and 5 stars. This rating reflects how reliably the app will recognise the image in real-world conditions. A 4 or 5-star image will scan consistently and quickly. A 1 or 2 star image may scan slowly or inconsistently. A 0 star image may not scan at all.

One critical rule: never use a QR code as your trigger image. If your printed piece includes an Artivive QR code for WebAR, upload a version of the image to the editor where the QR code area is covered or removed. The QR code should exist separately from the trigger image.

2.2 Optimising image recognition

Since the trackability rating directly affects your viewer's experience, it is worth understanding what drives it — and how to improve it when you get a low score.

What leads to good recognition:

  • High contrast between light and dark areas
  • Plenty of fine detail (textures, line work, intricate patterns)
  • Sharp, well-defined edges
  • Asymmetrical composition — no two quadrants of the image should look identical
  • A unique visual "fingerprint" — details that are not found in any other image in the Artivive database

What causes poor recognition:

  • Solid or near-solid color fields
  • Very little detail (flat, geometric, or minimalist designs)
  • Blurry or out-of-focus images
  • Highly symmetrical compositions (like a mandala or a perfectly mirrored design)
  • Too much text, especially repetitive text or standardized fonts that appear across multiple artworks

A note on the star rating: The stars are a guide, not a guarantee. An image can score 5 stars but scan poorly if it shares recognizable features with something already in the Artivive database. And occasionally a 3-star image will scan reliably in practice. Always test before printing.

If your image scores low: Try increasing contrast, adding texture or detail, cropping to a more visually interesting section, or adjusting the composition slightly. In many cases, small changes make a significant difference.

2.3 Adding and positioning digital layers

Once your trigger image is in place, the editor canvas becomes your workspace. You can now add the digital content that will appear when someone scans your artwork.

Adding layers: Click the plus button or the upload area to add your own files, or browse the elements library for pre-made assets. You can add:

  • Video files (.mp4, .gif, .mov) — for animation and motion
  • Image files (.jpg, .png) — for static overlays and graphic elements
  • Audio files — for music, narration, or sound effects
  • 3D object files (.glTF, .GLB) — for three-dimensional elements
  • Text layers — for titles, captions, or labels
  • Particle effect layers — for dynamic visual effects like sparks or smoke

Each file you add becomes a layer in your project, just like layers in a graphic design tool. Layers stack on top of each other and can be reordered.

Positioning layers: Use the on-screen transform handles to move, rotate, and scale each layer. For precise positioning, you can also type values directly into the Transform panel on the left side of the editor.

2D vs 3D view: The editor offers both a flat 2D view and a 3D perspective view. Use 2D view for precise positioning and alignment. Switch to 3D view to see how your layers sit in space — particularly useful if you're working with 3D objects or want to position elements that should appear to float above or behind the trigger image.

Layers that sit exactly on the trigger image plane will look like they are printed on the artwork. Layers positioned slightly above it in 3D space will appear to float. This depth dimension is one of the things that makes AR distinctly different from a simple video overlay.

2.4 Publishing and previewing your artwork

When your layers are in place and you are satisfied with the composition, it is time to publish.

Before you publish: Use Preview Mode, accessible via the button at the bottom center of the editor. Preview Mode simulates what a viewer will see when they scan your artwork in the real world. You can play through the experience, check timing, and verify that everything looks right. This is especially important if you are using Timeline animations or Sequences — both are interactive and require testing before you share.

If something looks wrong in Preview Mode, fix it in the editor and preview again. Do not skip this step.

Publishing: Click the Publish button, give your artwork a name, and confirm. The Artivive Editor will process your project — analyzing the trigger image, compressing assets, and preparing the experience for live delivery. This usually takes a minute or two depending on file sizes.

Once processing is complete, your artwork appears in your collection and is live. Anyone who scans the trigger image with the Artivive app will see your AR experience immediately.

Updating a published artwork: You can edit and republish at any time. Open the artwork's options menu (three dots), select Edit Artwork, make your changes, and publish again. If you are using WebAR, the QR code does not change — viewers who already have the link or code will automatically see the updated version next time they scan.

2.5 Viewing your AR experience

There are two ways to view an Artivive AR experience, and each has a different use case.

The Artivive App (iOS and Android): Download the free app from the App Store or Google Play. Open it and point your camera at the trigger image. No login is required to view artworks — the app is free and public. When you log in with your Artivive account, you get access to editing capabilities and AR Mode (covered in Module 3).

The app works with all published Artivive artworks, from anyone's account. It is the most reliable way to view, with the fullest feature support.

WebAR (browser-based): If you have published an artwork via WebAR (Pro Plus feature), viewers can scan a QR code with their device camera and the experience opens directly in their mobile browser — no app download required. This has significant benefits for exhibitions, events, and contexts where you cannot guarantee visitors will install an app.

WebAR has some limitations compared to the full app. In particular, Timeline animations are not currently supported in WebAR. If your artwork uses timeline animations, viewers will need the app. If your artwork uses static layers, images, video, and audio without timeline animation, WebAR works well.

Recording: Inside the Artivive app, tap the red record button to capture a video of the AR experience. The recording saves to your camera roll and can be shared directly to social media — a useful feature for promoting your work.

3.1 Supported file formats and size limits

Understanding file requirements before you start creating saves significant time. Artivive has specific requirements for each type of asset.

Trigger images: JPG or PNG. The longer dimension must be between 380 and 8,000 pixels. A high-resolution scan or export of your artwork works well — aim for at least 1,500 pixels on the longer side for reliable tracking.

Video layers: MP4, GIF, and MOV are all supported. The maximum file size per video is 100MB. You can have up to three unique video layers per project. Video files are compressed upon upload, so the final file served to viewers will be smaller than what you upload.

Image layers: JPG and PNG. For non-video layers combined (all images, 3D objects, and other non-video assets), the total must stay under 3MB on a Pro plan or 5MB on a Pro Plus plan. The trigger image itself does not count toward this limit.

3D objects: glTF and GLB are the recommended formats. OBJ and FBX also work. Files can be uploaded as a ZIP archive if they include separate texture files. The size limit is the same as for image layers: 3MB on Pro, 5MB on Pro Plus.

Audio: Standard audio files for sound layers. Audio can also be embedded in video files or in 3D objects.

Effect Layers:

A note on compression: Because all files are compressed on upload (except already-compressed 3D objects), you do not need to aggressively compress your source files before uploading. Upload quality originals and let the editor handle the optimisation.

3.2 Video best practices

Video is the most common and impactful layer type in Artivive. A well-crafted video layer can transform a static artwork into something genuinely magical. Getting the details right makes the difference.

Length: Keep videos under 45 seconds. This is not a technical limit — it is a usability principle. To view an AR experience, a viewer must hold their device up and point it at the artwork. After about 45 seconds, this becomes physically tiring and the experience shifts from enjoyment to endurance. For looping ambient animations, shorter is almost always better.

Transparency: One of Artivive's most powerful video features is transparent video. You can shoot or render a video against a green screen or solid color background, then use the color-picker tool to key out that colour. The result is a video that appears to float against the physical artwork rather than playing inside a rectangular frame. This creates a much more immersive AR effect. You can also upload a GIF with transparency already applied.

Sound settings: If your video has audio and you are creating an experience for an exhibition or public space, consider enabling the headphones-only mode via the headphone button in the layer settings. This ensures the audio only plays if the viewer is using headphones — avoiding disruption to other visitors. If sound is part of the experience, leave this off and let it play through the device speaker.

Format advice: MP4 with H.264 encoding works consistently across devices. Avoid obscure codecs. If your video looks fine on your computer but fails to play in the editor, re-export it as a standard MP4.

3.3 Working with 3D objects

Adding 3D objects to an AR experience creates a depth and dimensionality that flat video cannot match. A 3D model can be rotated, scaled, and animated — and in AR, it appears to exist in real physical space in front of the viewer.

Recommended format: GLB (binary glTF) is the best format for Artivive. It embeds all textures and materials into a single file, which means nothing gets lost in upload and the file stays well under the size limit. glTF (non-binary) also works but requires textures to be included in a ZIP archive.

OBJ and FBX are supported as fallbacks, but they are older formats with more compatibility quirks. If you have a choice, export as GLB.

Working in Blender: If you create or edit 3D models in Blender, export directly to glTF from Blender's built-in exporter. Choose Binary format to create a GLB file. Enable the Compression option to keep the file small. If your model has an animation, enable the Animation export option to include it.

Reducing file size: The most common challenge with 3D objects is keeping them under the file size limit. In Blender, use the Decimate modifier on your mesh to reduce polygon count — this maintains the general shape while dramatically reducing file size. You can also compress texture images in Photoshop or equivalent before embedding them. Flat color materials defined in Blender use no texture space at all, which is the most file-efficient approach.

Animated 3D models: 3D objects can include their own animation cycles (like a character walking or an object spinning). Upload them in a ZIP archive with all associated files. In the Timeline and Sequences features (Module 4 and 5), you can also trigger specific animations from a 3D model at defined moments.

3.4 Particle effect layers

Particle effects add dynamic, atmospheric motion to an AR experience — things like sparks, embers, smoke trails, falling leaves, dust clouds, or abstract floating elements. They are particularly effective for creating mood and atmosphere around a physical artwork without overwhelming it.

Adding a particle layer: In the editor, add a new layer and select the Particle Effect type. You will see a set of pre-made templates — fire, smoke, sparkle, and others — as well as a library of preloaded effect images. You can also upload your own image to use as the particle shape (for example, a custom logo, a petal, or a symbol relevant to your artwork).

Customising particle behavior: Each particle layer has a set of controls that define how particles move and appear:

  • Speed — how fast particles travel
  • Direction — which way particles move (up, outward from center, random, etc.)
  • Size — the scale of individual particles
  • Density — how many particles appear at once
  • Spread — how wide the emission area is

Experiment with these settings while watching the preview. Subtle, slow particle effects often work better with fine art — they add atmosphere without distracting from the work itself. High-density, fast effects are better suited to dynamic, energetic compositions.

Particle effects are CPU-intensive on mobile devices. If you layer multiple particle effects on top of each other, test on a mid-range device to make sure the experience still runs smoothly.

3.5 3D spatial audio

Standard audio layers in Artivive play at constant volume regardless of where the viewer is standing or how they hold their device. Spatial audio changes this — it creates a sense that sound is coming from a specific point in physical space, with volume that responds to the viewer's proximity and position.

The 3D Sound toggle in the layer settings unlocks two spatial audio modes, each with different behavior:

Logarithmic mode simulates the natural way sound behaves in physical space. Volume increases as you get closer, decreases as you move away. Crucially, the sound is never fully silent — even from a distance, you hear a faint presence. As the viewer moves into the inner sphere (the defined proximity zone), the audio reaches full volume.

Linear mode defines a hard boundary. Inside the outer sphere, volume scales linearly with distance. Outside the sphere, the sound is completely silent. This is useful when you want a sound to be clearly associated with a specific artwork — a viewer standing in front of a neighboring piece should hear nothing from yours.

Both modes allow you to adjust the size of the inner and outer spheres directly in the editor. Larger spheres mean the audio activates from further away. Smaller spheres create a more intimate experience.

When to use spatial audio: Spatial audio is most effective in multi-artwork exhibitions where multiple AR pieces exist in the same physical space. It prevents audio from one artwork from bleeding into another's experience. For single-artwork experiences, standard audio is usually sufficient.

4.1 What is Timeline and when to use it

Timeline is the animation engine built directly into the Artivive editor. Before Timeline existed, every layer in an Artivive artwork was static — it appeared when scanned and stayed in place. Video layers could play their own content, but the layers themselves could not move.

Timeline changes that. It lets you define, moment by moment, how each layer behaves over time — where it moves, how it rotates, when it appears or disappears, how it scales. The result is an AR experience where elements can drift, fly in, pulse, spin, synchronize with music, and choreograph together into a unified composition.

When to use Timeline:

  • When you want layers to animate in sequence (a title fades in, then an image slides in below it)
  • When you want elements to move continuously in a loop (a floating 3D object slowly rotating)
  • When you want to synchronize animations to music or a narration
  • When you want complex multi-layer choreography (a cinematic reveal)

When you might not need Timeline:

  • When a looping video layer already carries all the motion you need
  • When a static overlay is the right aesthetic choice

Timeline is a Pro Plus feature. If your account is on the free or Pro plan, upgrade before working through lessons 4.2 onward.

4.2 The five core Timeline actions

Open the Timeline panel by clicking the button at the bottom center of the editor. You will see the timeline interface — a horizontal sequence of tracks, one per layer, with a panel on the right showing available actions.

Every animation in Timeline is built from five core actions. Each action is added by clicking it or dragging it onto the timeline track for a specific layer.

Move To: Moves a layer from its current position to a new position over the duration of the action bar. The dotted line and blue preview in the editor show the layer's final position. Drag the blue preview to set where the layer will end up.

Rotate To: Rotates a layer by a specified number of degrees. You set the rotation amount in the action's settings. Useful for spinning elements, tilting objects, or adding subtle swaying motion.

Scale By: Resizes a layer by a multiplier. A Scale By value of 2 doubles the layer's size. 0.5 shrinks it to half. Use this for zoom effects, pulsing, or growing reveals.

Transform To: Combines Move To, Rotate To, and Scale By into a single action. Use this when you want a layer to simultaneously translate, rotate, and scale — which creates much more natural, organic movement than applying the three actions separately.

Show / Hide: Controls visibility at a specific moment. Unlike the other actions, Show/Hide has no duration — it is a point-in-time trigger. Place a Hide action at second 3 on a layer's track to make it vanish at exactly that moment. Use multiple Show/Hide actions on the same layer to create flickering, sequential reveals, or synchronized appearances.

Connecting actions to layers: When you add an action to the timeline, it appears with an orange icon, indicating it is not yet connected to a layer. Select the action, then use the "Layer to Affect" dropdown on the left to connect it to the layer you want to animate. Name your layers in advance — navigating a list of unnamed layers in a complex project is frustrating.

Adjusting speed: The length of an action bar on the timeline directly controls the duration of that action. Drag the right edge of a bar to make it longer (slower) or shorter (faster).

4.3 Speed curves and timeline duration

Speed curves define how an animation's velocity changes over its duration. They are the difference between movement that feels mechanical and movement that feels alive.

The speed curve is set per action in the action settings panel. Common curve types include:

  • Linear: Constant speed from start to finish. Good for mechanical, precise movements. Can feel robotic when overused.
  • Ease In: Starts slowly and accelerates toward the end. Good for objects gaining momentum.
  • Ease Out: Starts fast and decelerates. Good for objects coming to rest naturally.
  • Ease In/Out: Starts slow, peaks at full speed in the middle, then decelerates. This is the most natural-feeling curve for most animations.
  • Elastic: Overshoots the target position and bounces back before settling. Good for playful, bouncy effects.
  • Bounce: Similar to elastic but specifically simulates a physical bounce against a surface.

Experiment freely. There is no right or wrong curve for a given movement — it depends entirely on the feeling you want to create. A slow reveal with an Ease Out curve feels cinematic. The same reveal with Elastic feels playful.

Timeline duration is set at the top of the Timeline panel. This controls the total length of your animation loop. When the timeline reaches its end, it loops back to the beginning. If you set duration to 10 seconds, your animation plays every 10 seconds.

An important nuance: layers that are not connected to any Timeline action — like a video layer playing its own content — ignore the timeline duration entirely and loop on their own schedule. This means a 20-second video layer and a 10-second timeline can coexist, playing at their own rates independently.

4.4 Animating groups and combining actions

As your compositions become more complex, you will often want groups of elements to move together — a heading and its subtitle, a set of icons, or a cluster of decorative elements.

Creating groups: Click the "Create Group" option in the editor, then drag and drop the layers you want to group into it. Once grouped, you can apply Timeline actions to the group as a unit. All elements inside the group inherit the same motion and timing. Any new layers you add to the group later automatically adopt the group's animations.

Combining multiple actions on a single layer: A layer can have multiple Timeline actions applied to it simultaneously. For example, you can have a Move To and a Rotate To running at the same time on the same layer, creating a spinning drift effect. When two actions of the same type (like two Move To actions) overlap on the same layer, the one that ends later or sits on top in the timeline takes priority.

Transform To for natural motion: In the real world, objects rarely move in just one dimension. A falling leaf rotates and scales as it falls. A character reaching for something translates and rotates simultaneously. The Transform To action captures this multi-axis movement in a single step, giving you combined control over position, rotation, and scale with one unified speed curve. Use it whenever you want movement to feel organic rather than constructed.

Layering the Show action: The Show/Hide action is useful beyond simple appearance control. You can trigger sound layers at precise moments, cause visual elements to flash in sync with music, or build a sequential reveal where elements appear one after another. Time your Show actions carefully and use Preview Mode to verify the synchronization.

5.1 From linear animation to multi-state experiences

Linear animation has a clear limitation: it plays once (or loops) regardless of what the viewer does. The viewer is passive. Sequences changes that relationship.

A Sequence is a separate "state" of your artwork. Your artwork always starts in the Artwork Sequence — the idle state that plays by default when someone first scans your trigger image. You can leave this empty (showing a static image) or fill it with an animation.

From there, you can add as many additional sequences as you like. Each sequence is a fully independent animation timeline with its own layers, actions, and timing. Sequences can be triggered by viewer taps, by the completion of another sequence, or by a timer.

The conceptual shift: Instead of thinking "what happens when someone scans this artwork," start thinking "what are the different states this artwork can be in, and what moves it from one state to another." This is the same mental model used in game design and interactive media.

A simple example: an artwork starts in an idle state showing a gentle floating animation. When the viewer taps a specific element, a new sequence plays — perhaps a transformation, a reveal, or an entirely different visual world. When that sequence ends, the artwork returns to the idle state (or moves to another sequence, depending on your logic).

5.2 Creating and connecting sequences

Adding sequences: Open the Timeline panel. You are currently in the Artwork Sequence. Click the + icon next to the Sequences title to add a new sequence. Name it something descriptive — "Intro," "Tap Reveal," "End State" — rather than leaving it as Sequence 1, Sequence 2.

Animating within sequences: Each sequence has its own timeline. The same five core actions from Timeline (Move To, Rotate To, Scale By, Transform To, Show/Hide) are available. Each sequence also has its own Duration, controlling how long it plays before it reaches its end.

Connecting sequences via interactions: To link one sequence to another, you need to create a tap interaction. Select the layer that should be tappable. In the layer settings, add an On Tap interaction and choose Trigger Sequence from the interaction options. Then select which sequence that layer should trigger.

That layer is now interactive. When a viewer taps it in the AR experience, the corresponding sequence begins playing.

Loop vs Reset: When a sequence ends, you choose what happens:

  • Loop causes the sequence to replay continuously from the beginning.
  • Reset returns all layers affected by that sequence to their original state (their appearance before the sequence began).

Choose Loop for ambient states that should continue until something else triggers a change. Choose Reset for one-shot effects that should leave the artwork clean afterward.

5.3 Advanced sequence actions

Beyond the core animation actions, Sequences introduces three additional actions designed specifically for managing complex interactive logic.

Manage Sequence acts as a flow controller. Rather than waiting for a sequence to run to completion, you can place a Manage Sequence action on any timeline to play or stop another sequence at a defined moment. This lets you chain sequences together, create interrupts, or build timed progressions. For example: Sequence A plays for 5 seconds, then a Manage Sequence action at second 5 triggers Sequence B to begin — creating a seamless multi-part narrative.

Change Interaction lets you modify what happens when a layer is tapped, mid-experience. A layer can start with no interaction, then become a button that opens a link, then transition to triggering a new sequence. This means a single visual element can change its function as the experience progresses, creating richer interactive logic without adding more layers.

Play Animation is specifically for 3D models with multiple embedded animation cycles. It lets you trigger different animations from the same model at specific moments — for example, a character that stands idle by default, then waves when tapped, then walks when tapped again. Each Play Animation action can wait for the previous animation to finish (for smooth transitions) or interrupt it immediately (for responsive, reactive behavior).

An important behavior to understand: When a new sequence is triggered, it does not automatically stop other sequences that are already running. Multiple sequences can play simultaneously, layering their effects on top of each other. This is powerful — it allows you to build complex, multi-dimensional experiences — but it also means you need to consciously manage what is playing. Use Manage Sequence to stop sequences that should not continue running when new ones begin.

5.4 Using Preview Mode to test interactions

Interactive logic is hard to reason about without being able to test it. Preview Mode is essential for Sequences work.

Access Preview Mode via its dedicated tab in the editor. You will see your artwork rendered exactly as a viewer would see it. You can tap elements, trigger sequences, and watch the experience unfold. A notification appears each time a sequence starts or stops, giving you a real-time log of what is happening.

What to check in Preview Mode:

Does each tappable layer respond correctly? Tap every interactive element and verify that the right sequence triggers.

Do sequences end as intended? Watch through each sequence to its completion and confirm it loops, resets, or chains as configured.

Are there unintended overlaps? If you tap something and notice an unexpected layering of animations, you likely have sequences running simultaneously when they shouldn't. Use Manage Sequence to add stop actions at the right moments.

Does the timing feel right? Something that looks correct in the editor configuration can feel too fast or too slow in the actual experience. Adjust action bar lengths and test again.

Iteration is normal. Complex Sequences projects often require many rounds of Preview, adjust, Preview again. Build one connection at a time, test it, confirm it works, then build the next. Do not try to configure the entire experience and test it all at once — debugging a complex web of sequences is much harder than catching individual issues early.

5.5 What you can build with Sequences

Sequences unlocks a category of AR experience that goes well beyond animation: interactive, reactive, multi-path experiences. Here is a survey of what creators are already building with this feature.

Games and playable experiences: Sequences supports tap-based game logic. Tic-tac-toe, reaction games, memory puzzles, and choice-based interactions are all achievable. The On Tap interaction combined with Manage Sequence and Change Interaction gives you the building blocks for turn-based logic.

Musical instruments: Create an AR piano, drum pad, or musical instrument by mapping each note or sound element to a layer. Each layer triggers a sequence that plays a sound when tapped. Combine with spatial positioning to lay elements out like a real instrument.

Interactive video storytelling: Build a main video sequence that plays when the artwork is scanned, then offer tappable details that open new scenes. A portrait that comes to life might have tappable objects in the background that reveal backstory. Tapping the character's hands might trigger a different narrative path than tapping their eyes.

Choose-your-own-path narratives: Design sequences as story branches. The Artwork Sequence introduces a scenario. Two tappable elements lead to Sequence A or Sequence B. Each ends with new choices. The viewer navigates the story through their decisions.

Guided exhibition experiences: In a gallery or museum context, use sequences to add optional depth. The default state is clean and unobtrusive. A "tap to learn more" element triggers additional information, contextual imagery, or artist commentary — available for viewers who want it, invisible to those who don't.

The constraint is not technical — it is creative. If you can draw the logic diagram, Artivive can build it.
